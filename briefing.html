The Story
Markets Are Pricing Conflict and Tariff Shock as Temporary, Not Structural

The most important story this morning is not simply that markets recovered, but what they are implicitly saying about the durability of recent shocks. Reuters reported that Wall Street climbed back above pre-Iran-war levels even though oil remained sharply above pre-conflict levels and bond yields were still elevated. Simultaneously, the Trump administration is launching a refund system today for $166 billion in tariffs struck down by the Supreme Court — more than 56,000 importers have already completed steps tied to roughly $127 billion in electronic refunds. Two sources of market anxiety, war inflation and tariff friction, are being repriced from permanent impairment to transitional disturbance.

HIGH CONFIDENCE
The Mechanism

If investors believed higher oil and tariff instability would lock in sticky inflation, tighter financial conditions, and impaired margins, duration-sensitive growth would not be leading. Instead, tech is leading because the market believes three things: the war's worst energy scenario is less likely than feared; tariff damage is being partially reversed through refunds and legal constraints; and earnings upgrades, especially in AI-linked businesses, are outgrowing the macro drag. Reuters noted that 2026 S&P 500 earnings growth expectations actually improved since the war began. That is the tell.

Marks's Lens

Howard Marks' lens is the right one here because this is a classic second-level thinking setup. First-level thinking says: war raises oil, oil raises inflation, fewer rate cuts hurt tech, so risk assets should struggle. Second-level thinking asks what is already discounted and what is mispriced. Markets had already priced a lot of that fear in late March when the S&P nearly hit correction territory. Now the more important question is whether consensus has become too comfortable with a clean off-ramp. Marks would focus on the asymmetry: if the war truly fades and tariff refunds ease some pressure, current prices are rational; if energy spikes again or tariff instability persists into summer, today's calm leaves little margin of safety.

The Thread

The thread connecting today's tape: the market is choosing earnings over macro fear but has not fully erased the macro risk premium. Reuters showed stocks and the dollar largely retracing to pre-war levels while oil and parts of the rates complex still reflected inflation stress. The tariff refund rollout starting today reduces one source of policy friction and reinforces that the most aggressive trade shock was not durable. Add in ASML and TSMC both lifting outlooks on AI demand, and the real synthesis emerges: capital is rotating toward whatever still shows hard demand despite geopolitics.

Contradiction with prior thinking: Two months ago the cleaner view was that higher oil and fewer Fed cuts would mechanically crush long-duration tech. Instead, AI-linked earnings revisions have overwhelmed that headwind. Same macro headwind, different market response — because the earnings story is now dominant.
Also Moving
ASML and TSMC Confirm the AI Spending Boom Is Still Real

Reuters reported both companies lifted their 2026 outlooks — TSMC raising revenue guidance and stepping up capex while ASML increased its revenue forecast. The sharp takeaway: the AI buildout is still being funded at the infrastructure layer despite growing investor pressure on hyperscalers to prove returns. If the picks-and-shovels companies still see hard orders, NVDA's demand environment remains fundamentally supported. The risk is not demand collapsing tomorrow — it is the market eventually demanding proof that hyperscaler spending converts into durable monetization rather than perpetual capacity races.

HIGH CONFIDENCE
IMF Downgrade Did Not Break the Market's Risk Appetite

The IMF cut its global growth outlook to 3.1% and warned a worsening war scenario could push world growth toward 2.0%. Normally that downgrade paired with an energy shock would hit cyclicals and growth multiples together. Instead, markets treated the IMF baseline as manageable and focused on the fact that the fund did not radically rewrite the 2027 trajectory. Markets are using the IMF downside case as a tail risk reference point, not the base case. That keeps the burden of proof on bears: they need renewed oil acceleration, not just slower growth headlines.

MODERATE — watching whether IMF-style downside shows up in forward earnings revisions
